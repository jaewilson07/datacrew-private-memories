# Mister Car Wash — Sr. Manager, Business Analytics

**Position:** Sr. Manager, Business Analytics

**Company:** Mister Car Wash

**Location:** Tucson, AZ (Headquarters)

Hello!

The "Sr. Manager, Business Analytics" title caught my eye because it describes exactly what I did at Sony PlayStation — just with a different label and a very different industry.

At Sony, I built and led a Domo Center of Excellence from the ground up for our PaaS business. What started as a small team "keeping the lights on" for two or three business cases evolved into a program that positioned data as an accelerator across the organization. We bridged IT, Infosec, and Risk with business teams who needed data to make decisions — which is really what this role is asking for: someone who can translate complex data into executive-ready insights that actually drive action.

A few things from the job description that map directly to what I've done:

**Multi-unit operational analytics** — 550 locations across 21 states generating 90M+ washes annually creates massive data complexity. This is exactly the problem I solved at Sony, where we delivered reporting for 1,800+ users across global business units.  At SIE we implemented a robust content distribution model that served external and internal partners with variations of Domo Everywhere’s publish tooling – publish to a network of 3,000+ PlayStation publishing partners (external vendors) and colocate reporting assets from globally distributed internal developers into a handful of Domo distribution centers for internal consumption.  The challenges here were considerations around making sure that each Domo user only saw assets they were ‘supposed to see’ – while empowering departmental admins and developers with free reign to explore, mashup and develop their own data assets.  

Being transparent - I was more on the engineering side of designing and maintaining the architecture for how we would make these reporting assets safely available across the globe and other business analysts embedded in Sales, Marketing, Finance or Operations built the dashboards guided by requirements from their stakeholders.  As the hub in the COE model, my team and I were responsible for enabling and unblocking new analyst teams, by defining and best practices in collaboration with governance, risk, design and security teams, as well as facilitating the engineering behind more complex reporting or automation requirements.  Ultimately this was seen as a major unblocker because we kept report design in the hands of the analysts who knew the data best, and we partnered with engineering teams to keep them unblocked on technical implementation or governance and security hurdles.

**BI platform adoption and governance** — The job description says "drive adoption of BI platforms to automate reporting, streamline insights delivery, and enable advanced analytics capabilities."  At SIE, Domo initially felt like a shadow-IT purchase.  In the early days we did a lot of ‘skunkworks’ projects to rapidly prove value to business teams that were stymied with time constrained IT/IS-owned platform teams, but we quickly learned that scaling fast and safely meant partnering with experts instead of avoiding them. By the time I left, our COE had built monitoring workflows and governance tools that kept users safe while maximizing business teams' freedom to explore new workflows with data. 

I think it’s fair to say that having been a Domo Technical Consultant both out of the EMEA office and as a freelancer, I know Domo implementation deeply.  I used to maintain status as one of the top ranked Domo Community coaches in addition to having one of the longest running Domo-specific YouTube channels.  From my time at SIE as well as Jet Reports (a reporting and data warehousing software focused on Microsoft Dynamics ERPs) I have a strong sense of the business side of reporting and analytics as well.

**Leadership and cross-functional collaboration** — At SIE our COE team called ourselves "pretend-gineers" — we were generalist specialists who had to broker conversations between technical and non-technical stakeholders while also guiding delivery and standards. Early on I frequently felt the pressure to be "the smartest person in the room," but as the use cases expanded, I quickly realized I needed to find smarter people to drive initiatives. By the time I left, our goal to build generalists with several areas of specialist knowledge meant each specialist knew other true engineering and business unit specialists, and we were able to accelerate delivery across many areas. I love the generalist specialist model and suspect that an organization hiring at the intersection of analytics, finance, and operations recognizes that modern work is being reshaped by advancements in technology and AI.

**AI and advanced analytics** — I've been building AI automation workflows for the past year, including agentic AI systems for knowledge work and coding tasks. I'm comfortable articulating both the value and the limitations of these approaches to stakeholders who aren't data scientists. The job description mentions Python or R for advanced analytics — I use Python daily and have built predictive models, automated reporting pipelines, and AI-powered analytics tools. I believe this role recognizes an exciting opportunity to change expectations about how we conceptualize knowledge work to embrace new working and business models.

I believe my experience translates to this role. I'd welcome the conversation to see if we're a good fit!

Thank you for your time, Jae Wilson
